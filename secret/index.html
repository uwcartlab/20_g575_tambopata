Participatory Zoning Goals

Participatory zoning processes like the one we re-enacted are underway in scores of protected areas in the tropics where biodiversity overlaps with poverty and management institutions are weak and underfunded. Ideally, participatory zoning balances ecosystem conservation and economic development goals across large areas and among diverse stakeholders. Community mapping can bolster traditional resource use claims. Zoning can also highlight areas of special importance for protecting water and wildlife.

So, in sum, why go through all the effort to engage stakeholders and do participatory zoning?

  • To balance conservation and development goals at the landscape scale.
  • To bring competing interest groups together to negotiate rules and create a shared sense of landscape.
  • To encourage greater stakeholder buy-in and avoid costly top-down enforcement.

What happened in Tambopata in real life?

Participatory zoning has been underway in one form or another in Tambopata for nearly two decades. Some past participatory zoning efforts have focused on agricultural development or mining outside protected areas. Our re-enactment matches an effort launched in 2001 that was funded by the Macarthur Foundation and led by Peruvian conservationists. In reality, more than a dozen zoning maps were proposed by various stakeholder groups over the course of years. We selected and simplified four of these proposed maps to use in this exercise.

Given that this exercise was funded and led by conservationists, groups considered ‘forest friendly’ were privileged from the start, namely Brazil nut harvesters, ecotourism operators, ecologists, the Federation of Indigenous peoples (FENAMAD), and the Agrarian Federation (FADEMAD). However, the latter group (FADEMAD, the Agrarian Federation) was relatively large and powerful and its members included some miners and loggers. Thus, after several round tables and debates, participants chose proposal #2, a simplified version of the map originally proposed by FADEMAD. This map was thought to best balance competing interests. Notably, in place of the buffer zone, the zoning participants favored creating a new zoning category (a ‘Community Reserve’) that allowed all kinds of activities, but only if they were conducted by Tambopata residents. Miners were allocated the gold-rich riverbanks along the Malinowski in the NW section of the Reserve.

This consensus map was sent for approval to the central offices of Peru’s Institute of Natural Resources (INRENA) in Lima, where it sat for a year under consideration. When INRENA finally sent the formally approved zoning map back to Tambopata, stakeholders were met with a surprise: INRENA designated a large Buffer Zone, arguing that ‘Community Reserve’ was not a legally recognized land use zone. INRENA also erased the direct use zone along the Malinowski river meant for mining, hoping this would protect the source of water for the growing city of Puerto Maldonado from mercury contamination. Additionally, in 2010 the Peruvian Government established a formal mining corridor where mining could legally take place with formal permits; however, mining continues to occur outside of this legally designated area.

Local reaction to the final, INRENA-approved map was mixed. Indigenous people were disappointed but not surprised that their ancestral lands were not expanded. Some Ese’eja citizens were pleased to see ecotourism granted big areas, other Ese’eja opposed this. Brazil nut harvesters were encouraged by the formal public support of their activity. The zoning exercise made them feel more confident in their rights to exclude loggers and farmers from the old growth forest where they harvest Brazil nuts, but they admit hunting is not controlled. Most participants believed mining on the Malinowski would not stop even if it was forbidden on the zoning map. Similarly, many participants felt the Buffer Zone was only a designation on paper, not one that would be enforced in reality.

  3. Stepping out of character, do you think Peruvian conservationists leading this exercise made the right choice to limit the voice of mining, industrial agriculture and logging in the zoning process for the Reserve?
  4. Based on your experience in this activity, in a sentence or two, describe a few opportunities and challenges to taking a participatory approach to zoning.

The mixed results of Participatory Zoning at Tambopata

Did the participatory zoning exercise succeed at balancing conservation & development at the landscape scale?

Failures: Despite the zoning effort, some illegal logging continued in the northeastern corner of the TNR and neighboring indigenous territories. Worse, as expected, illegal gold mining activities exploded in the Buffer Zone and along the upper Malinowski River. In 2018 alone, mining destroyed over 9,000 hectares of forests (that’s about 18,000 football fields).

The Monitoring of the Andean Amazon Project (MAAP) has put together time lapse images that show how gold mining (and related deforestation) has rapidly expanded in the Alto Malinowski and La Pampa (and other regions) between 2016-early 2019. Click here to see them (scroll down to La Pampa, and the Alto Malinowski).

Successes: Forests in the Tambopata National Reserve (TNR) are still fairly well protected. There is negligible mining in the Reserve (despite the presence of gold deposits). Instead, more forest-friendly activities predominate (Brazil nut harvesting and tourism). Moreover, the adjacent one million-hectare Bahuaja-Sonene park is mostly protected (due to its remoteness). Importantly, keep in mind that, unlike Tambopata, most participatory zoning in the tropics happens where there is no huge, intact national park next door. The social and ecological stakes of participatory zoning are higher when there is no other forested area left for conservation or sustainable use.

Did the participatory zoning exercise succeed at bringing competing groups together to negotiate rules and creating a shared sense of landscape? Did it avoid conflicts & costly, top-down enforcement?

Failures: Conservation continues to be a politically charged issue at Tambopata. Some defiant leaders of loggers and miners have been elected to public office by running their campaigns on anti-conservation messages. The explosion of illegal gold mining ultimately required expensive, militarized enforcement (see next).

Successes: Although participatory zoning didn’t ‘win over’ the entire population of the region to a conservation mindset, it raised public awareness. Prior to the exercise most residents knew little about the TNR or its purpose, whereas after, many had a better understanding of the TNR’s conservation objectives. In particular, locals had a better awareness of Brazil nut and tourism concessions, the location of the Buffer Zone, etc. Most notably, this activity helped to strengthen a new Local Management Committee made up of zoning exercise participants and lead by the former Agrarian Federation (FADEMAD) leader. Together they have achieved remarkable impact, e.g. helping the press and civic organizations to publicly protest mercury pollution, creating an anonymous ‘hotline’ to report mining infractions, and blowing the whistle on corruption in the mining sector. Confronting mining is very risky given the enormous profits and criminality of many operations. Management Committee leaders regularly receive death threats. This Committee is also working with government agencies to improve the mining formalization process. So effective is this committee, that the Peruvian government has asked them to coach management committees around other protected areas. The government is even considering expanding the designated area of the Buffer Zone at Tambopata, assuming the Local Management Committee can extend its good work. Committee members are concerned about this expansion given they already feel overwhelmed with their current work.

If you would like to learn more about the Tambopata Local Management Committee and/or contribute to their cause, you can find them here: https://www.tambopatas.org/ [Website in Spanish]

In Feb 2019, Peru’s government launched ‘Operation Mercury,’ a multisector governmental operation to confront the illegal mining crisis in this region. This crackdown has significantly slowed gold mining deforestation in the Buffer Zone and kept it from penetrating the Tambopata National Reserve and the Bahuaja Sonene National Park.

Did the participatory zoning exercise improve local leadership & technical planning skills?

Successes! Stakeholders’ involvement in the exercise allowed them to build new institutions to support decision making. Many zoning participants became members of a steering committee to oversee the buffer zone (see note above about the Local Management Committee).

Did the participatory zoning exercise protect areas of special ecological importance?

Mixed Results: The Malinowski River, an important headwater, was severely damaged by mining despite zoning protection. Most forest is protected in the Tambopata Reserve and the Bahuaja-Sonene Park.

Challenges & Uncertainties of Participatory Zoning efforts

HIGHLY POLITICAL?
Yes, very! Zoning is not just about where resources will be used, but who gets access.

SLOW?
Yes, this was a >5-year process. The government then spent more than a year after the roundtable formalizing and adjusting the map chosen at the round table. Zoning is still adjusted every couple of years, but each time, it builds on previous zoning efforts and concerns a smaller area.

DEMOCRATIC?
Was Tambopata zoning coercive or democratic? Miners and loggers were not even invited to the roundtable because conservationists thought their activities were not compatible with TNR goals. (What do YOU think, should miners have been invited?). Some indigenous people felt the exercise was a waste of time.

INEFFECTIVE?
Note that pluralistic processes like participatory land use zoning tend to favor ambiguous rules for zones (e.g. the community reserve zone). Why do you think this is true? Ambiguity makes subsequent enforcement difficult. Also, in this case (like many others), donors funded the zoning exercise (US$ 500k total) but did not provide funds for subsequent enforcement. Why not?

TAKEAWAY: Participatory zoning is an example of community-engaged conservation. It is slow, politically fraught work, but key to enduring land stewardship. Also remember, local zoning efforts are inadequate to defend land from activities like mining. Controlling a high-profit, highly destructive activity like mining requires both community engagement and interventions from government agencies.
