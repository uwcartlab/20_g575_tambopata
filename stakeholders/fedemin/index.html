Tambopata

Leader of the Mining Federation (FEDEMIN)

Photo credit: John Poole, NPR

Miguel Yallico is disgusted by the poverty he sees in Tambopata, especially because he knows there are millions of dollars of gold lying buried in the region’s soils. Miguel mines gold in three places in Tambopata, but like 90 percent of local miners, he operates illegally. He is not afraid of punishment because Peru’s government agencies lack personnel to fully enforce mining regulations. Besides, Miguel has many powerful connections, and he usually gets advance warning when an enforcement event is imminent.

Miguel is thrilled that gold prices are rising and more people are coming to Tambopata to strike it rich. But he’s troubled that labor conditions for miners in Tambopata have gotten more dangerous in recent years. Corruption and violence have soared, and he directly blames the Peruvian government and its Ministry of Energy and Mines. He argues that the high cost of the Ministry’s mining permit process, the slowness of the bureaucracy, and the endless red tape make local miners resort to working illegally.

Miguel’s fiery public speeches decrying the government and opposing environmental regulations have made him popular and politically powerful. When asked about the environmental impacts of mining, he fights back. He accuses environmentalists of indifference to the poor and of being beholden to hegemonic interests of global northern countries.
