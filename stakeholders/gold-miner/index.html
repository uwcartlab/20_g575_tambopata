Small-Scale Gold Miner

Photo credit: John Poole, NPR

Pedro Gomez is a long-time resident of Tambopata and a gold miner who has been mining the banks of the Malinowski river for 20 years. Pedro first turned to gold mining when his family was in an economic crisis and he desperately needed to make some cash fast. Like many of his mining friends, he calls himself an 'artisanal' Artisanal mining: small-scale mining preformed by individuals, often on an informal/unofficial basis. technology used has lower impact than industrial mining. miner because he uses only modest equipment. He does not have a formal (legal) mining title or permit. Pedro submitted the initial paperwork for a permit to Peru’s Ministry of Energy and Mines, but he can’t afford to pay for the environmental impact assessment a legal permit would require. As a result, Pedro, like the vast majority of the miners he knows, works at the margins of the law. Since Pedro has been operating illegally, the military has destroyed his mining equipment twice as part of their crackdown on illegal mining in the region. Now he must work for another miner who buys bigger equipment with the aid of crime syndicates. He is alarmed by increasing violence in mining areas and sees that other old-timer artisanal miners are arming themselves and taking turns at guard when they are at the riverbanks. Alan leads FADEMAD, a grassroots political organization that represents ~5000 small farmers in the Madre de Dios region. FADEMAD’s goal is to improve the quality of life of its members, both long-time residents and more-recently settled colonists. Alan believes that poor farmers’ quality of life depends on a healthy environment. Thus, he tries to defend local farmers AND the forest. Mining has grown in Tambopata in recent years, and larger-scale miners have begun to push artisanal miners as well as farmers off their land with threats of violence. Given the rising global demand for gold, Pedro thinks it is impossible to stop mining in Tambopata and that the government should focus on regulating the activity and helping local residents benefit from the activity instead of outsiders. Pedro is disturbed by news reports that mining is contaminating the region’s water and fish, yet he is proud to be a miner. He just wishes he could work independently, with no fear of violence, and that he could afford to use lower polluting mining technology.
