Wildlife Ecologist:

Dr. Natalia Ortiz is a Peruvian scientist who studies the ecology of Giant River Otters (Pteronura brasiliensis) in Tambopata, one of the last strongholds for this endangered species. She thinks Giant Otters are magical animals – for one, they are enormous (up to 6 feet long) and live in tight-knit social groups that collectively raise pups. They fish cooperatively and are very playful. As top predators, they require large areas of intact ecosystems to survive. Natalia is deeply worried that the giant otters in Tambopata are threatened by the same human activities that have wiped the species out from other parts of the Amazon. Mining and agriculture are the worst threats, but giant otter populations are also highly sensitive to hunting and to overfishing, which is depleting fish stocks in the rivers they call home. Moreover, her research shows that the decline of giant river otters signals that many of Tambopata’s natural aquatic habitats are in trouble. The draining of wetlands and lakes for mining and agriculture threatens many species, and it also releases significant amounts of carbon.

Natalia is aware that tourists pay a lot of money to see giant river otters, but she also knows that more local people in more places need to benefit from eco-tourism in order for communities to protect the environmental conditions necessary to sustain viable populations of otters. It also might be possible to justify the conservation of palm swamps (an important habitat for giant otters) because they yield Mauritia flexuosa fruits (aguaje), a popular fruit sold in Amazonian towns. But hunting and fishing in the swamps would have to be strictly controlled for otters and other wildlife to persist. Or perhaps another way to sustain otter habitat is by protecting the forest through forest carbon projects that pay environmental stewards like Brazil nut harvesters to conserve their land. Last, she has tried to convince people to save otter habitat as a means to protect the sources of clean drinking water. Ultimately she is troubled when people describe wild animals simply as ‘resources’ or rely only on utilitarian arguments. She sees otters, other wildlife, and natural forests as having deep intrinsic value, deserving protection beyond human interests. She sees hope in the rising concern for environmental protection among Peruvian citizens, including those living in large cities like Lima. She Is fighting to protect wildlife for all Peruvians, including future generations.
