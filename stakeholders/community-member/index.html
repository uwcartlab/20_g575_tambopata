Indigenous Ese’eja Community Member:

Ramón Mishaja is from the Ese’eja Indigenous Territory of Infierno, an indigenous community with recognized land rights lying adjacent to the Tambopata National Reserve. The Ese’eja were originally a nomadic community living in the region around Tambopata for centuries. They have deep ecological knowledge and a strong spiritual connection to Amazonian ecosystems. Though individual communities like the one at Infierno won legal communal title to certain lands in 1974, the Ese’eja have lost access to many of their sacred sites and to areas they once relied on for hunting, fishing, and gathering. Today the Ese’eja Indigenous Territory of Infierno covers only 9,558 hectares; far less than their original homeland.

Ramón’s primary concern is that his people are losing rights to their ancestral lands. Like other Ese’eja, he’s unhappy that the Peruvian government and other outsiders are making decisions about their ancestral lands without consulting leaders in the Federation of Native Peoples (FENAMAD). Ramón is adamant that indigenous people should control their own destiny.

He is also thinking about different ways that his home Ese’eja community in Infierno can secure more income. His community has an eco-tourism partnership with the tourism company Rainforest Expeditions. The Ese’eja own the tourism lodge built by the company in Infierno, and Rainforest Expeditions handles the marketing and management. Together, they share the profits. Some of Ramon’s relatives have good jobs working at the lodge. But other Ese’eja distrust tourism companies to equitably share tourism revenue. They think it might be better to follow the example of the Harakmbut indigenous community living in an indigenous territory called Kotzimba (also adjacent to Tambopata Reserve). Harakmbut residents are mining gold throughout their Territory using heavy machinery and mercury. Although the damage is severe, they have become wealthy from mining. Alternatively, Ramón has heard rumors about forest carbon projects associated with an international climate change mitigation mechanism known as REDD+ (Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation). Bosques Amazónicos, a Peruvian conservation company, has REDD projects that pay communities to store carbon in the forests of Tambopata National Reserve, but he does not know the contract conditions or if the contract will benefit native communities. Meanwhile, Ramón is troubled by reports that a third Tambopata-adjacent Ese’eja Indigenous territory to the east (Palma Real) has suffered invasions by outside miners and loggers, some of whom are violent.
