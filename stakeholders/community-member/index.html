Ramon Mishaja is from the Ese’eja Indigenous Territory of Infierno, The largest group of indigenous people in the region, numbering ~600 today, some estimate ~10,000 before 1890. Glossary an indigenous community with recognized land rights lying adjacent to the Tambopata National Reserve. The Ese’eja were originally a nomadic community living in the region around Tambopata for centuries. They have deep ecological knowledge and a strong spiritual connection to Amazonian ecosystems. Though individual communities like the one at Infierno won legal communal title to certain lands in 1974, the Ese’eja have lost access to many of their sacred sites and to areas they once relied on for hunting, fishing, and gathering. Today the Ese’eja Indigenous Territory of Infierno covers only 9,558 hectares, A 100 x 100 m unit of land (1 hectare = 2.3 acres). Abbreviated “ha". Most Tambopata farms are ~40 ha. Glossary an area significantly smaller than their original homeland.

Ramon’s primary concern is that his people are losing rights to their ancestral lands. Like other Ese’eja, he’s unhappy that the Peruvian government and other outsiders are making decisions about their ancestral lands without consulting leaders in the Federation of Native Peoples (FADEMAD). Leader of the Agrarian Federation(FADEMAD): Alan leads FADEMAD, a grassroots political organization that represents ~5000 small farmers in the Madre de Dios region. FADEMAD’s goal is to improve the quality of life of its members, both long-time residents and more-recently settled colonists. Alan believes that poor farmers’ quality of life depends on a healthy environment. Thus, he tries to defend local farmers AND the forest. Click FADEMAD to learn how Alan weighs the costs and benefits of land management practices of his varied constituents are interested in pursuing. Ramon is adamant that indigenous people should control their own destiny.

He is also thinking about different ways that his home Ese’eja community in Infierno can secure more income. His community has an eco-tourism A form of nature-based tourism in which tourist impacts on the environment are mitigated. Glossary partnership with the tourism company Rainforest Expeditions. Co-Founder of Rainforest Expeditions: Oscar is passionate about biodiversity conservation but believes that saving the rainforest must be profitable to be successful. To this end, he built Rainforest Expeditions, a high-end ecotourism company. Click Rainforest Expeditions to learn how Oscar collaborates with other interest groups to conserve the rainforest and bring economic support to the area while grappling with other challenges in participatory land use management. The Ese’eja own the tourism lodge built by the company in Infierno, and Rainforest Expeditions handles the marketing and management. Together, they share the profits. Some of Ramon’s relatives have good jobs working at the lodge, but other Ese’eja distrust tourism companies to equitably share tourism revenue. They think it might be better to follow the example of the Harakmbut indigenous community living in an indigenous territory called Kotzimba (also adjacent to the Tambopata National Reserve). Harakmbut residents are mining gold throughout their territory using heavy machinery and mercury. Although the damage is severe, they have become wealthy from mining. Alternatively, Ramon has heard rumors about forest carbon projects associated with an international climate change mitigation mechanism known as REDD+ Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation, is an incentive-based mechanism for reducing greenhouse gas emissions through improved forest management in developing countries. Glossary (Reduced Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation). Bosques Amazónicos, A for-profit organization facilitating a REDD+ project in Tambopata. Click BAM for more information. a Peruvian conservation company, has REDD projects that pay communities to store carbon in the forests of Tambopata National Reserve, but he does not know the contract conditions or if the contract will benefit native communities. Meanwhile, Ramon is troubled by reports that a third Tambopata-adjacent Ese’eja Indigenous territory to the east (Palma Real) has suffered invasions by outside miners and loggers, some of whom are violent.
